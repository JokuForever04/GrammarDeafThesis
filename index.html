Grammar Overview

Grammar is the foundation of effective communication. It provides the rules that allow us to structure our thoughts and ideas in a clear, understandable way...

  • Sentence Structure: The arrangement of words to create meaningful sentences.
  • Verb Tenses: How verbs express the timing of an action (past, present, future).
  • Parts of Speech: The roles of words in a sentence, such as nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs.
  • Punctuation: Marks like commas, periods, and semicolons that help clarify meaning.

Grammar Tips

  • Subject-Verb Agreement: Ensure that the subject and verb match in number (singular/plural).
  • Use Correct Punctuation: Proper punctuation marks like commas, periods, and apostrophes are vital in creating clear sentences.

Grammar Examples

  • She is going to the store. (Correct verb tense: present continuous)
  • He likes apples, bananas, and oranges. (Correct comma usage in a list)

Advanced Grammar Concepts

  • Conditional Sentences: Example: "If I had known, I would have helped."
  • Relative Clauses: Example: "The man who helped us was very kind."
  • The Subjunctive Mood: Example: "If I were you, I would study more."

GrammarDeaf Help Center

1. Subject-Verb Agreement

Click to see the answer

Answer: Subject-verb agreement means the subject and verb in a sentence must agree in number (singular or plural).

  • Use "is" with singular subjects: The cat is sleeping.
  • Use "are" with plural subjects: The cats are playing.
  • If the subject is a collective noun, it may be treated as singular or plural based on context: The team is winning.

2. Apostrophes for Possession

Click to see the answer

Answer: Apostrophes show possession in nouns. Here's how to use them:

  • For singular nouns, add an apostrophe + "s": The dog's bone.
  • For plural nouns ending in "s," add only an apostrophe: The dogs' bones.
  • For plural nouns not ending in "s," add an apostrophe + "s": The children's toys.

3. Using "Its" vs. "It’s"

Click to see the answer

Answer:

"Its" is a possessive form of "it," meaning something belongs to it: The cat chased its tail.

"It’s" is a contraction of "it is" or "it has": It’s raining outside. (It is raining outside.)

4. Using Commas in Lists

Click to see the answer

Answer: Commas are used to separate items in a list of three or more elements.

Example: I bought apples, bananas, oranges, and grapes.

Note: The comma before "and" is optional and known as the Oxford comma. It's more common in formal writing.

5. Pronoun Agreement

Click to see the answer

Answer: Pronouns must match the gender and number of the noun they replace:

  • Use "he" for a singular male subject: John went to his car.
  • Use "she" for a singular female subject: Sarah forgot her keys.
  • Use "they" for a singular or plural subject when gender is unknown or not specified: Taylor lost their phone.
  • Note: “They” can also be used as a singular pronoun when referring to someone who prefers gender-neutral language.

6. Sentence Fragments

Click to see the answer

Answer: A sentence fragment is an incomplete sentence. It typically lacks a subject, verb, or both.

Example of a fragment: Running in the park.

This is not a complete sentence because there is no subject (who is running?).

To correct it:

Complete sentence: She is running in the park.

7. Using "Fewer" vs. "Less"

Click to see the answer

Answer:

Use "fewer" for things you can count individually (countable nouns): I have fewer books than you.

Use "less" for things that cannot be counted individually (uncountable nouns): I have less water than you.

8. Run-on Sentences

Click to see the answer

Answer: A run-on sentence occurs when two or more independent clauses (complete sentences) are improperly joined without proper punctuation or a conjunction.

Example of a run-on: I went to the store I bought some milk.

To fix it:

  • Add a period to separate the two sentences: I went to the store. I bought some milk.
  • Or use a comma with a conjunction: I went to the store, and I bought some milk.
